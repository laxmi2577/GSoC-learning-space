## Why Mesa
Large Language Models are brilliant, but they are inherently stateless, text-bound, and prone to hallucination. To build truly autonomous systems, LLMs need a deterministic grounding layer—a physics engine for their thoughts. Mesa is the premier framework for exactly that. I was drawn to Mesa because its grid environments, schedulers, and data collectors provide the perfect scaffolding to turn floating LLM chatbots into spatially aware, goal-oriented agents that can actually *do* things.

## What I want to learn
I want to move beyond prototype scripts and learn how to engineer open-source software at scale. Through my work on Mesa-LLM so far, I have started diving deeply into asynchronous concurrency (`asyncio`), native tool calling, and Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG). By contributing to Mesa, I want to master the art of writing highly optimized, maintainable Python code that other researchers and developers can rely on without breaking their existing simulation pipelines.

## Where I want to go
My goal is to successfully integrate native LLM capabilities into the core Mesa framework for GSoC 2026. Beyond the summer, I see myself transitioning into a high-level Data Science or AI/ML Engineering role where I design autonomous, multi-agent systems for enterprise applications. Becoming a core contributor to a repository as prestigious as Mesa is the exact proving ground I need to bridge the gap between academic prototypes and production-grade engineering.
